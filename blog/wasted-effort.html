Are you wasting your time?

So many of the things I've worked on have never been used. From data platforms, to learning frameworks, to entire apps.

What if we could identify huge wastes of time before they happen?

First, what's the name for one of these projects/frameworks/initiatives? What's it called when someone puts loads of new processes in place, creates loads of new tools, or defines a way of working, and none of it ever gets used?

I've seen many names for this, but I like 'Shelfware' the best. It doesn't sugar coat it, but it doesn't imply imcompetence either.

It's misleading though, because it sounds like it has to be a product, whereas these initiatives can be anything — a new process, a new way of working, a new tool, etc.

Shelfware does exactly what it's supposed to, but nobody wants to use it.

Why does this happen?

Everyone falls into the same trap; we see an opportunity to improve a process and immediately think about generalising and scaling up the solution so that it can apply to multiple people, and be used multiple times, thereby fixing the problem forevermore.

In reality, no problem is that generic, and no solution is that universal.

The truth is, 99% of initiatives are a poor use of time. Why?

1. Comfort zone

People will choose to work on something because it's in their comfort zone, but still looks impressive enough that it justifies their role. Developing a new tool or framework is well within our comfort zones, because we don't have to talk to anyone or change how we work. We can just get on with building it.

We limit ourselves to solutions that we are comfortable building. This causes us to unavoidably pick a solution before understanding the problem, and regardless of whether it's the right one.

2. Framework Fallacy

We naturally believe that working within a framework is always 'good practice', but it actually leads to lock-in, bureaucracy, and a lack of creativity.

3. Vanity

Creating something so that you can put your name on it and say you did it feels good. We all do this when we're feeling insecure about our roles. This can even extend to spending; the more we spend on our project, the more important and justified it seems.

4. Building something good is hard :(

Worthwhile initiatives are hard and require a wide range of skills that no one person has, but are not nessesarily time that consuming.

5. Creating value takes patience

Sometimes, you have to do something today that pays off in 3 years — like planting a seed to grow a tree. People avoid long-term initiatives, even if they're easy wins, because they want the credit now. Financial sturcturing of projects doesn't help with this either; you can't get paid to work on a 3 year project without a clear ROI within the first year at least.

So what does a worthwile initiative even look like?

Some of the most high value activities aren't labled as 'projects' at all. Somebody passionate just gets on with it. To them, the size of the problem and the benefit of the solution are obvious and provide all the motivation they need.

Imagine someone saying "Hey everyone, today we're kicking off Project Zeus: I'm going to show Joe from sales how to load all his contacts into Salesforce. It will take 10 minutes."

That's not a project. That's just helping someone. But the value per unit time is astronomical compared to a Shelfware initiative.

What's the antidote?

Here's my advice:

1. Focus on the users — solve the right problem

Remember these tried and tested rules for building good products that people actually want to use from various product and UX gurus, but apply them all of your initiatives too — no matter how small.

  1. Don't build anything without answering the question: "What problem does it solve?"
  2. Don't build anything without answering the question: "Would people use this if it worked perfectly? Do they have any incentive to?"
  3. Don't build anything without answering the question "Why don't we just..." — investigate the obvious solution to your problem, like calling up your customer and asking them to stop doing the thing that's causing the problem, or using an existing off-the-shelf tool.
  4. Don't build anything if someone hasn't asked for it. Don't be frustrated when they ask for something completely different.
  5. Give your users a wireframe or even a 5 minute 'paper prototype' first to validate the new user experience you're creating.
  6. Invest time in gathering feedback from your users, understanding their specific pain points, and following up to find out why they are or aren't using your solution.
  7. Systems thinking: look at the whole ecosystem to understand why these problems arise. Is your solution addressing the root cause or a secondary problem that shouldn't exist in the first place?
  8. Find the quickest possible solution to the problem, no matter how imperfect it is. Prove the value of solving the problem before you try and solve it 'properly'.
  9. Understand that every unique situation is different and requires a different approach. Therefore, you don't need a framework. You need multiple tools and methods.
  10. Accept that you may not have the skills to solve this problem. It can still be solved, but you're not getting the credit. Swallow your pride and get help.
  11. Get an outside perspective — someone who can tell you "this is stupid and a waste of time". You won't get this from your friends or colleagues.
  12. Rely more on the people side: training and retrospective discussions will gradually improve processes rather than tools that 'fix' them.
  13. Don't build tools that remove functionality and flexibility from a process (this is especially hard when everyone's using Excel).
  14. Don't be afraid to say no to stakeholders.

2. Solve a specific problem well

Be wary of frameworks and platforms that try to solve lots of people's problems once and for all:

  1. Only build frameworks after learning which bespoke tools are useful and verifying the need for the framework.
  2. Take a neutral stance towards frameworks in general: they are not automatically going to improve things, you do not need a framework for everything.
  3. Build quick and simple solutions to immediate problems, not platforms.
  4. Reverse engineer the platform later once you've felt the pain of not having it. It sounds stupid, but I've built too many platforms that get no use whatsoever. The sensible middle ground is to have an idea in mind for how you would design the platform and transition to it later so that you don't have to re-engineer too many things.
  5. Prefer methodologies and loose protocols to strict frameworks.

3. Put down the shovel

If you suspect you may be working on some Shelfware currently, here's what I'd suggest:

  1. Meet with your users, and try to get to the bottom of what their problems really are.
  2. Retire any tools or features that aren't getting used, and see if your users complain.
  3. Pick a flagship use case for your platform (one that clearly demonstrates a step change in productivity or capability), and focus on building that first, even if the platform ends up being useless for other use cases.
  4. Reduce the number of people responsible for product decisions down to one. Make sure they're motivated by adding value for users, not pleasing stakeholders.
  5. Adopt a "fail fast" approach — be prepared for most of your initiatives to 'fail', and abandon them quickly.

4. Learn from other people's mistakes

Every failed product is an opportunity to gain some wisdom, and there are thousands of these everywere we go.

Products that didn't actually solve a problem:

Products that failed the "Why don't we just..." test

  • The Juicero Press was a $400+ Wi-Fi-connected juicer. Customers could just as easily squeeze the proprietary juice packets by hand.

Great idea, bad execution:

  • Google Stadia worked perfectly... if you were one of the few people with high speed, extremely low latency, and highly reliable internet... I actually think this would succeed if they advertised it a bit more, and had some good games.
  • The Krusty Burger on an unmanned oil rig from The Simpsons is a prime example — great product, but not convinient enough.

Tools that removed functionality:

  • The Apple Vision Pro is an incredible feat of engineering, but (ignoring the high price) if you had one, you wouldn't actually want to use in public (embarassing), or around friends/family (rude), or for long periods of time (can't spend more than 2 hours away from a power source), or for anything that didn't require you to wear a big, heavy, nausia inducing hat, hence why Apple have now pivoted to smart glasses instead. It's obvious in hindsight that this wasn't going to catch on, but perhaps they could have determined this sooner by role playing a day in their life with the product.

5. Revisit this question regularly

As the project goes on, keep asking yourself: "is this one big waste of time?", along with all of the questions above. Bookmark this page if you found it useful, and use it as a quick checklist.

But is the time really wasted... if you had fun?

No, it's not a waste of time if you had fun!

Many of my projects failed to become popular because they started off as 'something cool' — a solution looking for a problem. I work on things for fun and for learning, so it doesn't matter that they didn't turn into anything. At least I didn't waste time promoting them or creating loads of training materials.

I will openly admit that projects like bb are not useful enough to justify the amount of time spent on them. I don't expect them to be widely used, and I don't promote them. They're just fun to work on.
